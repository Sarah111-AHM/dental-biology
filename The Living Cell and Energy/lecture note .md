# 1. The Cell as an Energy Factory

## Concept
The living cell functions as a **miniature chemical factory**, conducting thousands of chemical reactions.

## Function
- Extracts energy from molecules  
- Uses energy to perform work  

## Example
Some organisms convert energy into light via **bioluminescence**  
(e.g., fireflies, deep-sea jellyfish).

# 3. Metabolic Pathways

## Definition
A metabolic pathway is a **series of enzyme-catalyzed reactions** that transform a specific starting molecule into a final product.

## Structure Example

| Step | Enzyme   | Substrate → Product |
|------|----------|---------------------|
| 1    | Enzyme 1 | A → B               |
| 2    | Enzyme 2 | B → C               |
| 3    | Enzyme 3 | C → D               |

## Key Concept
- Each step requires a **specific enzyme**

# 4. Catabolic Pathways

## Definition
Catabolic pathways **break down complex molecules into simpler compounds**, releasing energy.

## Example: Cellular Respiration
- Breakdown of **glucose** in the presence of **oxygen**
- Produces **energy** for the cell to use

# 1. Anabolic Pathways

## Definition
Anabolic pathways are metabolic processes that **consume energy** to construct complex molecules from simpler ones.

## Example: Protein Synthesis
- Building **proteins** from **amino acids**

## Key Concept
- Opposite of **catabolism**
- Requires **energy input**

# 3. Forms of Energy

## Energy
Energy is the **capacity to cause change**.

## Key Forms

### 1. Kinetic Energy
- Energy associated with **motion**
- **Example:** Moving muscles, flowing water

### 2. Heat (Thermal Energy)
- A type of kinetic energy from **random atomic or molecular motion**
- **Example:** Body temperature

### 3. Potential Energy
- Energy stored due to **position or structure**
- **Example:** A rock at the top of a hill

### 4. Chemical Energy
- Potential energy stored in **chemical bonds**
- Released during **chemical reactions**
- **Example:** Glucose breakdown in cellular respiration

# 1. Thermodynamics and Energy Transformation

## Definition
Thermodynamics is the study of **energy transformations**.

## Types of Systems

### Closed System
- Isolated from surroundings
- No exchange of energy or matter  
- **Example:** Liquid in a thermos

### Open System
- Exchanges energy and matter with surroundings  
- **Example:** Living organisms

## Key Concept
Organisms are **open systems**, constantly exchanging energy and matter.

---

# 2. The First Law of Thermodynamics

## Definition
The energy of the universe is **constant**.

## Principle
Energy can be **transferred and transformed**, but **cannot be created or destroyed**.

## Alternate Name
Law of **Energy Conservation**

## Example
- Chemical energy in food  
  → Kinetic energy for muscle movement  
  → Heat energy released

---

# 1. The Second Law of Thermodynamics

## Definition
During every energy transfer or transformation, some energy becomes **unusable**, usually lost as **heat**.

## Principle
Every energy transfer or transformation **increases the entropy (disorder) of the universe**.

## Key Concept
**Entropy** = measure of disorder.

---

# 2. Energy Transformation in Living Cells

## Fact
Cells inevitably convert **organized forms of energy** into **heat**.

## Spontaneous Processes
- Occur **without energy input**
- Can be **fast or slow**
- Must **increase the entropy of the universe** to occur


## Example
- Diffusion of molecules in the cytoplasm
- Breaking of chemical bonds releasing heat

---

# 3. Biological Order and Disorder

## Cellular Order
Cells build **highly ordered structures** from less ordered materials  
(e.g., proteins, organelles).

## Organismal Energy Flow
- Energy enters ecosystems as **light** (from the sun)
- Energy exits ecosystems as **heat**

## Key Concept
Life maintains **local order** but contributes to **global disorder**.

---

# Complexity and the Second Law

## Clarification
The evolution of more complex organisms **does not violate** the second law of thermodynamics.

## Reason
- Entropy may **decrease locally** within an organism
- But the **total entropy of the universe increases**

## Key Concept
Local order is possible **at the expense of increasing global disorder**.

# 2. Free Energy and Spontaneous Reactions

## Definition
The free-energy change (**ΔG**) of a reaction predicts whether a reaction occurs **spontaneously** or requires **energy input**.

## Importance for Biologists
- Determine which reactions occur **without energy input**
- Identify reactions that **require added energy**

## Example
- **Spontaneous:** Glucose breakdown in cellular respiration (ΔG < 0)
- **Non-spontaneous:** Protein synthesis from amino acids (ΔG > 0)

# Free Energy in Metabolism

**Concept:**  
Free energy (ΔG) applies directly to biochemical reactions in living systems.

**Importance:**  
Helps predict whether reactions occur spontaneously or require energy input.

---

## Exergonic and Endergonic Reactions

### Exergonic Reactions
- **Definition:** Reactions that release free energy.
- **Spontaneity:** Spontaneous (ΔG < 0)
- **Example:** Cellular respiration — breakdown of glucose.

### Endergonic Reactions
- **Definition:** Reactions that absorb free energy from the surroundings.
- **Spontaneity:** Nonspontaneous (ΔG > 0)
- **Example:** Protein synthesis from amino acids.

---

## Quick Memory Tip
- **Entropy:** Local decrease possible, universe total ↑
- **ΔG:** Negative = spontaneous, Positive = energy needed

9. Enzyme Kinetics & Michaelis-Menten
